Navigation & Mega Menu Redesign — Goal, Evidence & Validation Plan

Author: Andres G., UX Designer Prepared: June 16, 2026 Audience: Design, Product/Catalog & Engineering stakeholders Status: For review
The one-look summary

What this is, in four lines

The problem
A diagnosed findability problem in the nav — oversized, inconsistent menus, no scope cues, and hover friction — not a cosmetic refresh.
The goal
Reduce navigation friction so users find the right category faster and more directly. Modernized look is a secondary benefit.
The response
Concept 7 (progressive-reveal panel) + a search-aligned taxonomy staged as a Level 1→3 roadmap. Every decision traces to evidence.
How we'll prove it
Two studies — tree testing + moderated usability — measured against today's structure as a built-in baseline.
Why a goal, when the menu was never tracked? The current mega menu has no interaction data — so we can't run a classic before/after on analytics. But the problem was still diagnosed, through expert evaluation and adjacent behavioral data. That diagnosis is the goal. The success baseline is created inside the validation study by testing today's structure alongside the new one.
01 · The problem we set out to fix

This is a findability fix, not a refresh

Three structural problems and three high-impact usability defects were identified before any design work began. They define the goal — and they're why this is a measurable usability project, not a subjective restyle.

Structural — Current-state audit

The menu's architecture is inconsistent

  • Grouped vs. flat lists mixed across menus (Heating is structured; Plumbing, HVAC, Electrical, Valves, Fittings are flat)
  • Subcategory volume swings from ~13 (Valves) to ~42+ (Fittings) — a column sized for one looks broken for the other
  • Duplicate items across menus (Tools, Valves, Fittings, Pumps, Gas Cocks) with no canonical home
Usability — Baymard expert evaluation

Three confirmed, severity-scored defects

  • Hover delay — sibling menus flash open on diagonal cursor moves. Impact 2.0; observed in 100% of test users
  • No current-scope indicator — users can't tell which category they're in. Impact 1.8
  • Sub-types as categories, not filters — users can't compare across types. Impact 1.7

Together these are a triangulated problem statement. A cosmetic refresh has none of this evidence behind it — we have named defects, severity scores, and behavioral corroboration. Current-State Audit · Baymard × Amplitude Synthesis

03 · The goal that drove the design

One goal, three levels — so every decision has a "why"

Business goal
Make browsing a reliable path to purchase. Reduce reliance on search-as-rescue and lost demand (e.g. HVAC terms with no nav home), so more sessions convert through the catalog.
Experience goal
Reduce the findability friction the audit diagnosed. Users should locate the right category faster, more directly, and with clear sense of where they are — across both trade-pro and homeowner mental models.
Design principles
The rules each proposal had to satisfy: (1) reduce scanning cost in large menus, (2) make current scope always visible, (3) remove timing/hover friction, (4) speak the user's vocabulary, (5) let high-intent users filter, not drill.

Read top-to-bottom this is the rationale chain: the business reason → the experience outcome → the concrete rules that generated the designs in Section 05.

04 · Goals → Signals → Metrics (HEART)

How success is defined without a historical baseline

We use the Google HEART / Goals-Signals-Metrics model because it forces the goal first, then asks what would prove it. The primary category — Task Success — needs no analytics history at all: it's generated inside the study, against today's structure as the comparison.

HEARTGoalSignalMetricMeasured in
Task Success Primary Users find the right category quickly and directly Fewer wrong turns and backtracks; faster correct selection Task success rate · directness · time-to-task Tree test + usability (vs. current-state arm)
Happiness Users feel confident about where they are and where to go Higher perceived ease; less hesitation/confusion Single-Ease Question (per task) · SUS (overall) Moderated usability sessions
Engagement Post-launch The menu becomes a real discovery path, not a fallback to search Menu opens lead into category → product → cart; search-as-rescue drops Menu open rate · reveal depth · search-as-fallback rate · category→PDP funnel New event tracking, after launch
The headline we'll be able to report isn't "X% engagement" (no before exists) — it's "task success rose from N% on today's structure to N% on the new one, with fewer recovery clicks." That comparison is manufactured inside the study, which is exactly what solves the no-baseline problem.
05 · The design response & its rationale

What we proposed — and the evidence behind each move

Direction: Concept 7, a progressive-reveal panel (orient → narrow → select), was advanced as the primary direction. Taxonomy: the menu structure is framed as a three-level roadmap by amount of catalog work — Level 1 (reorder + add missing high-search items, fix labels), Level 2 (named groupings where lists are long or mixed), Level 3 (resolve cross-department duplicates and canonical homes). The current prototype renders the Level 2 grouped target with an adaptive column layout. Nav bar (a complementary proposal): the mega menu is the primary scope, but introducing Shop All as its entry point naturally opens the door to a leaner header. Header Option 2 reduces the bar to two entry points — Shop All (browse by department) and Shop by Job — with every department living inside the Shop All panel. It's a secondary idea, included here because it follows the direction larger e-commerce and trade-distribution sites are already taking with a consolidated "Shop All" entry. 06 · How we'll validate — two studies

The lightweight plan: structure, then interaction

The two big unknowns are different in kind, so each gets the method that fits it. Together they cover both risk axes without a heavy research program. The live A/B test is deferred to post-launch monitoring — it needs the build plus tracking and isn't a gate.

Study 1 · Validates structure

Tree testing

Answers: is the taxonomy right? Is B worth its cost over C?

UI-free findability test of the bare category hierarchy. Fast, remote, quantitative — the single highest-leverage study, because the taxonomy choice is the most expensive and least reversible decision.

  • Test three trees: current · Level 1 (reorder + add) · Level 2 (grouped) on the same tasks — does grouping earn its catalog cost?
  • The current tree is your synthetic baseline — the before/after analytics never captured
  • Metrics: success rate, directness, time
  • Tools: Treejack / Optimal Workshop or Lyssna
Study 2 · Validates interaction

Moderated usability test

Answers: does the panel work in the hand? Per segment?

Behavioral observation on the prototype — the only way to test click-to-open, column reveal, scope highlighting, and the keyboard path. Also reveals whether "Shop by Job" gets used or routed around.

  • 5–8 sessions per segment — trade pros and homeowners run separately (their mental models diverge)
  • Open each session with a first-click task so that signal isn't lost
  • Metrics: SEQ per task, SUS overall, observed success/recovery
If forced down to one: tree testing. Getting the taxonomy wrong is the costliest mistake — it drives the catalog team's content roadmap — so it's the decision most worth de-risking before build.
07 · Open questions for stakeholders

Decisions this plan needs from the room

Product / Catalog
Approve the Level 1→3 taxonomy roadmap and commit to the staged catalog content work. Confirm the Level 1 additions + Level 2 groupings close the HVAC vocabulary gap, or flag where more depth is needed.
Design / Kristine
Confirm Header Option 2 — a nav bar of just Shop All + Shop by Job, with all departments inside the panel. Trade-off to weigh: a cleaner, consolidated header versus losing the always-visible category spine that repeat and new-to-market users rely on today.
Design / Research
With the in-panel breadcrumb removed, confirm how the current-scope cue (Baymard #3) is delivered — is persistent active-state highlighting sufficient, or is a lightweight scope label still needed? Worth an explicit task in the usability test.
Research / All
Confirm task success + directness as the primary success metric, and agree to test today's structure as the baseline arm in the tree test.
Engineering
Commit to shipping the mega-menu event schema with the redesign, so the post-launch Engagement metrics in Section 04 are actually measurable.
Scope
Run both studies, or start with tree testing alone given appetite for a lightweight research stage?
